ECONOMIC SIMULATION - ALGORITHM DOCUMENTATION
===============================================

This document describes the economic models and algorithms used in the
economic-simulation.html file. The simulation models a generic western
nation's macroeconomy on a quarterly time step, drawing on several
well-established open-source economic frameworks.

1. OVERVIEW AND TIME STEP
=========================
Each simulation turn represents one fiscal quarter. Statistics that are
naturally annual (e.g., GDP growth rate) are annualized from the quarterly
change for display purposes. The formula used is:

    annualized_growth = ((GDP_t / GDP_{t-1}) - 1) * 400

where the factor of 400 converts a quarterly ratio to an annualized
percentage (multiply by 4 for quarters, by 100 for percent).


2. SOLOW GROWTH MODEL (Potential GDP)
=====================================
Reference: Solow, R. (1956). "A Contribution to the Theory of Economic
Growth." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 70(1), 65-94.

Potential GDP represents the economy's maximum sustainable output given
its resources. It grows each quarter according to three factors:

    g_potential = labor_growth + capital_contribution + TFP_growth

Implementation details:

  a) Population and labor force (logistic growth):
     Population follows a logistic model with carrying capacity K=120M:
       qPopGrowth = (baseRate / 4) * max(0.1, 1 - (P/K)^1.5)
     This produces S-curve demographics: fast growth at low population,
     decelerating as the nation approaches carrying capacity.

     Labor force participation is a quadratic function of population
     density (popRatio = P/K):
       participation = 0.50 + 0.04*popRatio - 0.08*popRatio^2
     Participation rises slightly at low density (urbanization gains)
     then declines at high density (aging demographics, congestion).
     Clamped to [0.42, 0.55].

  b) Capital accumulation:
     - Investment is fixed at 20% of real GDP (investment share).
     - Capital depreciates at 2% per year (0.5% per quarter).
     - K_t = K_{t-1} * (1 - 0.005) + (Investment / 4)

  c) Total Factor Productivity (TFP):
     - Baseline TFP growth is 0.5% per year (0.125% per quarter).
     - A stochastic shock of magnitude +/-0.1% is added each quarter.

  d) Capital contribution (cubic polynomial with diminishing returns):
     Instead of the linear Cobb-Douglas approximation, the capital
     contribution uses a cubic polynomial in the investment-to-capital
     ratio (x = I_quarterly / K):

       capital_contrib = 0.45*x - 1.2*x^2 + 1.0*x^3

     At low x: nearly linear growth (0.45*x dominates).
     At moderate x: diminishing returns kick in (-1.2*x^2).
     At high x: the cubic term (+1.0*x^3) provides a slight recovery,
     modeling economies of scale in very capital-intensive phases.
     This captures the empirical observation that the first units of
     capital investment are highly productive, but returns diminish.

  e) Potential GDP update:
     - potentialGDP_t = potentialGDP_{t-1} * (1 + labor_growth
                        + capital_contrib + TFP_growth)


3. IS CURVE (Aggregate Demand / Output Gap)
===========================================
Reference: Hicks, J. R. (1937). "Mr. Keynes and the Classics: A
Suggested Interpretation." Econometrica, 5(2), 147-159.

The IS curve determines the output gap using polynomial policy
transmission channels. All three channels use non-linear forms:

  a) Fiscal stance (asymmetric quadratic):
     rawFiscal = (gov_spending_target - prev_expenditures) / prev_realGDP

     If rawFiscal >= 0 (stimulus):
       fiscal = 0.5*f - 2.0*f^2      (diminishing returns)
     If rawFiscal < 0 (austerity):
       fiscal = 0.5*f - 3.0*f^2      (accelerating contraction)

     This asymmetry means: moderate stimulus is effective but large
     stimulus has diminishing impact (crowding out), while austerity
     is contractionary and becomes more so at larger magnitudes.

  b) Monetary stance (quadratic):
     rDev = centralBankRate - 3.0  (deviation from neutral)
     monetary = -0.004*rDev - 0.0008*rDev*|rDev|

     The quadratic term means extreme rate deviations (very loose or
     very tight) have outsized impact on demand. At 6% above neutral,
     the effect is roughly 60% larger than a linear model predicts.

  c) Tax drag (quadratic with cross-term):
     iTaxDev = (incomeTaxRate - 25) / 100
     eTaxDev = (exciseTaxRate - 10) / 100

     taxDrag = -(0.20*i + 0.10*e + 0.80*i^2 + 0.40*e^2 + 0.50*i*e)

     The quadratic terms (i^2, e^2) capture the accelerating
     disincentive effect of high tax rates. The interaction term
     (i*e) models the compounding drag when multiple taxes are high
     simultaneously — the combined burden exceeds the sum of parts.

  d) Output gap persistence (state-dependent AR):
     The autoregressive parameter is itself a function of the gap:

     If prevGap < 0 (recession):
       persistence = 0.75 + 0.05*|prevGap|  (recessions are sticky)
     If prevGap >= 0 (expansion):
       persistence = 0.65 - 0.05*prevGap    (booms fade faster)

     Clamped to [0.5, 0.85]. This captures the empirical observation
     that recessions exhibit hysteresis while booms self-correct
     (capacity constraints, tightening policy).

     outputGap_t = persistence * prevGap + (1-persistence) * target

  e) Actual GDP:
     realGDP_t = potentialGDP_t * (1 + outputGap_t)


4. PHILLIPS CURVE (Inflation)
=============================
Reference: Phillips, A. W. (1958). "The Relation between Unemployment
and the Rate of Change of Money Wage Rates in the United Kingdom,
1861-1957." Economica, 25(100), 283-299.

Also: Friedman, M. (1968). "The Role of Monetary Policy." American
Economic Review, 58(1), 1-17. (Expectations-augmented version.)

The simulation uses a non-linear New Keynesian Phillips Curve:

    inflation_t = inertia + demand_pull + cost_push + expected_inflation

  a) Inflation inertia (quadratic self-reinforcement):
     inertia = 0.60*pi_{t-1} + 0.05*pi_{t-1}*|pi_{t-1}|/10

     The quadratic term means high inflation becomes self-reinforcing
     (wage-price spiral), while low/moderate inflation has standard
     persistence. At 10% inflation the inertia coefficient effectively
     rises from 0.60 to 0.65.

  b) Demand-pull inflation (cubic polynomial):
     g = outputGap
     demand_pull = 3.0*g + 8.0*g^2*sign(g) + 15.0*g^3

     Linear term (3.0*g): standard Phillips curve slope.
     Quadratic term (8.0*g^2*sign(g)): inflation accelerates faster
       at large positive gaps (capacity constraints, bottlenecks).
       The sign(g) preserves correct direction for negative gaps.
     Cubic term (15.0*g^3): extreme gaps produce very large price
       movements (hyperinflation risk / deep deflation).

     At gap = +2%: demand_pull = 6.0 + 3.2 + 1.2 = 10.4%
     At gap = -2%: demand_pull = -6.0 - 3.2 + 1.2 = -8.0%
     (asymmetric: inflation rises faster than it falls)

  c) Cost-push shocks:
     cost_push = random_shock(magnitude = 0.15)

  d) Inflation expectations (polynomial de-anchoring):
     piDev = |inflation_{t-1} - 2.0|
     anchorStrength = max(0.05, 0.20 - 0.02*piDev - 0.005*piDev^2)
     expected_inflation = 2.0 * anchorStrength

     When inflation is near 2%, expectations are well-anchored
     (anchorStrength ~ 0.20). As inflation deviates, the anchor
     weakens quadratically — capturing the empirical finding that
     prolonged off-target inflation erodes central bank credibility.
     At 6% deviation, anchorStrength drops to ~0.05 (near-zero weight
     on the target).

  e) Floor: Inflation is bounded below at -3% (deflation limit).

  f) Price level update:
     priceLevel_t = priceLevel_{t-1} * (1 + inflation_t / 400)

  g) Nominal GDP:
     nominalGDP = realGDP * priceLevel / 100


5. OKUN'S LAW (Unemployment)
=============================
Reference: Okun, A. M. (1962). "Potential GNP: Its Measurement and
Significance." Proceedings of the Business and Economics Statistics
Section, American Statistical Association, 98-104.

Unemployment uses a cubic polynomial in the output gap, with a
hysteresis term for prolonged downturns:

    Let negGap = -outputGap

    unemployment_t = NAIRU
                   + 2.0 * negGap              (linear Okun)
                   + 1.5 * negGap * |negGap|   (quadratic amplifier)
                   + 0.8 * negGap^3             (cubic for extremes)
                   + hysteresis + shock

  a) NAIRU: Set at 5.0%.

  b) Linear term (2.0 * negGap):
     Standard Okun's Law — 1% below potential adds 2pp unemployment.

  c) Quadratic amplifier (1.5 * negGap * |negGap|):
     Deep recessions cause disproportionate job losses (mass layoffs,
     firm closures). The |negGap| preserves sign correctness.

  d) Cubic term (0.8 * negGap^3):
     Extreme gaps (e.g., financial crises) have devastating employment
     effects. At -3% gap: cubic adds 0.8*(-27) = -21.6pp to the base.

  e) Hysteresis term:
     If prev_unemployment > NAIRU + 2:
       hysteresis = 0.3 * (prev_unemployment - NAIRU - 2)
     Else: hysteresis = 0
     Prolonged high unemployment damages skills, networks, and labor
     force attachment, making it harder to recover — a well-documented
     effect in European labor markets post-2008.

  f) Bounds: Clamped to [1.5%, 25%].

  g) Employment:
     employed = laborForce * (1 - unemploymentRate / 100)


6. INTEREST RATE DETERMINATION
==============================
Reference: Taylor, J. B. (1993). "Discretion versus Policy Rules in
Practice." Carnegie-Rochester Conference Series on Public Policy,
39, 195-214.

The simulation models three interest rates, all influenced by a
polynomial sovereign risk premium:

  a) Sovereign risk premium (cubic polynomial):
     d = debtToGDP - 0.40   (deviation from 40% baseline)

     sovereignRiskPremium = max(-0.5, 1.0*d + 2.0*d*|d| + 3.0*d^3)

     Linear (1.0*d): baseline risk response.
     Quadratic (2.0*d*|d|): risk accelerates at moderate debt levels.
     Cubic (3.0*d^3): crisis-level risk at very high debt.

     At debt/GDP = 60%: srp = 0.20 + 0.08 + 0.024 = 0.30%
     At debt/GDP = 80%: srp = 0.40 + 0.32 + 0.19 = 0.91%
     At debt/GDP = 100%: srp = 0.60 + 0.72 + 0.65 = 1.97%
     At debt/GDP = 120%: srp = 0.80 + 1.28 + 1.54 = 3.62%

     The convexity means debt crises escalate rapidly once triggered.
     Sovereign wealth (negative d) gives a modest discount, capped
     at -0.5%.

  b) Central bank policy rate:
     - Directly set by the player via the "Central Bank Rate" slider.
     - The player effectively acts as the central bank governor.

  c) Home mortgage interest rate (quadratic credit spread):
     u = unemploymentRate - 5.0
     credit_spread = 2.5 + 0.10*u + 0.02*u^2
                     + sovereignRiskPremium + shock(0.1)

     The quadratic term (0.02*u^2) means credit conditions tighten
     sharply in high-unemployment environments as banks face rising
     default rates (non-linear credit risk).
     Minimum mortgage rate floored at 1.0%.

  d) Commercial bank prime rate (quadratic sovereign pass-through):
     primeSovereign = srp + 0.3*srp*|srp|
     prime_rate = centralBankRate + 2.0 + primeSovereign + shock(0.05)

     The sovereign risk premium is amplified quadratically when
     passed to the prime rate, reflecting that commercial banks
     face compounding funding costs during sovereign stress.
     Minimum prime rate floored at 0.5%.


7. BUSINESS INVENTORY CYCLE
============================
Reference: Blinder, A. S. & Maccini, L. J. (1991). "Taking Stock: A
Critical Assessment of Recent Research on Inventories." Journal of
Economic Perspectives, 5(1), 73-96.

Business inventories use a non-linear partial adjustment model where
the speed of adjustment is itself a quadratic function of the gap:

    inventory_trend = realGDP * 0.15
    invGap = (trend - prev_inventories) / prev_inventories
    adjustSpeed = clamp(0.15 + 0.30*invGap^2, 0.05, 0.50)
    adjustment = (trend - prev_inventories) * adjustSpeed
    inventories_t = prev_inventories + adjustment + shock(2)

  - Target inventory-to-GDP ratio is 15%.
  - Base adjustment speed is 15% of the gap per quarter.
  - Quadratic acceleration (0.30*invGap^2): firms correct large
    imbalances faster (rush orders when stockouts loom, fire sales
    when overstocked). Adjustment speed can reach 50% for big gaps.
  - Small imbalances are corrected gradually (minimum 5% speed).


8. GOVERNMENT REVENUE MODEL
============================
Total government revenue uses polynomial models incorporating
Laffer curve effects and demand elasticity:

  a) Income tax (quadratic Laffer curve):
     taxable_income = nominalGDP * 0.55
     iRateDev = (incomeTaxRate - 25) / 100
     effective_rate = (incomeTaxRate/100) * (1 - 0.8*iRateDev^2)
     income_tax_revenue = taxable_income * max(0, effective_rate)

     The Laffer term (1 - 0.8*iRateDev^2) reduces the effective tax
     take when rates deviate far from the 25% baseline. At 50%
     nominal rate, the effective rate is ~50% * (1 - 0.05) = 47.5%.
     At extreme rates, behavioral responses (evasion, reduced labor
     supply) erode the tax base.

  b) Capital gains tax (polynomial asset model + Laffer):
     Asset growth uses a bivariate quadratic with interaction:
       g = realGDPGrowth / 400
       r = (5 - centralBankRate) / 100
       assetGrowth = 1.0 + 1.2*g + 3.0*g^2
                     + 1.5*r + 4.0*r^2 - 2.0*g*r + shock

     Quadratic in growth (3.0*g^2): bull markets amplify gains.
     Quadratic in rates (4.0*r^2): low rates inflate asset bubbles.
     Cross-term (-2.0*g*r): interaction dampening — strong growth
     with low rates partially offset (overheating triggers correction).

     Laffer effect on capital gains:
       cgRateDev = (capitalGainsTaxRate - 15) / 100
       effective_cg_rate = (rate/100) * (1 - 1.2*cgRateDev^2)
     Capital gains are more elastically responsive to tax rates than
     income (coefficient 1.2 vs 0.8), as investors can defer
     realization.

  c) Excise tax (quadratic consumption elasticity):
     exciseRatio = exciseTaxRate / 100
     consumptionShare = max(0.35, 0.60 - 0.5*exciseRatio^2)
     consumption = nominalGDP * consumptionShare
     excise_tax = consumption * exciseRatio

     The consumption share of GDP shrinks quadratically as excise
     taxes rise, modeling price elasticity of demand. At 25% excise
     rate, consumption share drops from 60% to ~57% of GDP. This
     limits the revenue gain from very high excise rates.

  d) Total revenue:
     govRevenues = income_tax + capital_gains_tax + excise_tax


9. GOVERNMENT EXPENDITURE MODEL
================================
Government spending uses polynomial automatic stabilizers and
debt-aware borrowing costs:

  a) Discretionary spending:
     base_spending = govSpendingLevel / 4  (quarterly)
     - The player sets the annual spending target (default 410 billion).

  b) Automatic stabilizers (cubic polynomial):
     uExcess = max(0, unemploymentRate - NAIRU)
     auto_stabilizer = 3.0*u + 0.8*u^2 + 0.1*u^3

     Linear (3.0*u): standard unemployment benefits.
     Quadratic (0.8*u^2): emergency programs, extended benefits.
     Cubic (0.1*u^3): crisis spending (bailouts, emergency relief)
       kicks in during severe downturns.

     At 2pp above NAIRU: stabilizer = 6.0 + 3.2 + 0.8 = 10.0B/qtr
     At 5pp above NAIRU: stabilizer = 15 + 20 + 12.5 = 47.5B/qtr
     The cubic escalation reflects real-world crisis responses.

  c) Debt service (sovereign-risk-adjusted):
     effectiveBorrowRate = centralBankRate + max(0, sovereignRiskPremium)
     debt_service = govDebt * (effectiveBorrowRate / 100) / 4

     The government borrows at the policy rate plus the sovereign
     risk premium (from Section 6a). This creates a non-linear
     feedback loop: high debt raises the risk premium, which raises
     borrowing costs, which increases spending, which increases debt.

  d) Total quarterly expenditure:
     quarterly_spending = base_spending + auto_stabilizer + debt_service
     - Annualized (* 4) for display.


10. FISCAL BALANCE AND DEBT DYNAMICS
=====================================
  a) Government surplus/deficit:
     surplus = govRevenues - govExpenditures
     - Positive values indicate a surplus; negative values a deficit.

  b) Current deficit:
     deficit = govExpenditures - govRevenues
     - Displayed explicitly on the dashboard. Shows the deficit amount
       when spending exceeds revenue, or "None (Surplus)" otherwise.

  c) Debt accumulation:
     quarterly_deficit = (govExpenditures - govRevenues) / 4
     govDebt_t = govDebt_{t-1} + quarterly_deficit
     - Debt grows when the government runs a deficit.
     - Debt shrinks when the government runs a surplus.

  d) Sovereign wealth:
     - When government debt crosses below zero (i.e., cumulative
       surpluses have exceeded all prior debt), the balance is
       relabeled as "Sovereign Wealth" in the dashboard.
     - The debt-to-GDP ratio card becomes "Sovereign Wealth-to-GDP".
     - This reflects the real-world concept of sovereign wealth funds
       (e.g., Norway's Government Pension Fund).
     - Sovereign wealth provides a rate discount: the sovereign risk
       premium turns negative (see Section 6a), reducing mortgage
       and prime rates below their baseline spreads.

  e) Debt-to-GDP ratio:
     debt_ratio = |govDebt / nominalGDP| * 100
     - Displayed as a percentage; flagged as concerning above 80%.
     - When debt is negative, displays as "Sovereign Wealth-to-GDP".

  f) Debt-interest feedback loop:
     - High debt raises the sovereign risk premium (Section 6a).
     - Higher market rates increase debt service costs (Section 9c).
     - Higher debt service worsens the deficit, further increasing debt.
     - This creates a potentially destabilizing positive feedback loop,
       consistent with real-world sovereign debt crisis dynamics.


11. STOCHASTIC SHOCKS AND RANDOM EVENTS
========================================
The simulation includes two categories of randomness:

  a) Continuous shocks:
     The shock(magnitude) function returns a uniformly distributed random
     value in the range [-magnitude, +magnitude]:
       shock(m) = (Math.random() - 0.5) * 2 * m
     These are applied to: population growth, TFP, output gap, inflation
     (cost-push), unemployment, interest rate spreads, inventories, and
     asset growth.

  b) Discrete random events (checked each quarter):
     - 4% chance: Trade disruption (reduces real GDP by 0.2%)
     - 3% chance: Technology productivity boost (raises potential GDP by 0.1%)
     - 2% chance: Consumer confidence surge (raises real GDP by 0.2%)
     - Remaining quarters: "Steady economic conditions"

  c) Threshold-triggered events:
     - GDP growth < -1%: Contraction warning
     - GDP growth > 5%: Rapid expansion notice
     - Inflation > 5%: Elevated inflation warning
     - Inflation < 0%: Deflation warning
     - Unemployment > 8%: High unemployment alert
     - Unemployment < 3%: Tight labor market notice
     - Debt-to-GDP > 80%: Fiscal sustainability warning
     - Mortgage rate changes > 0.5pp: Rate shift notice


12. INITIAL CONDITIONS
======================
The simulation begins with conditions representative of a stable,
mid-sized western economy:

    Real GDP:               2,000 billion
    Potential GDP:          2,000 billion (no output gap)
    Price level:            100 (base index)
    Population:             50 million
    Labor force:            25 million (50% participation rate)
    Employed:               23.75 million (5% unemployment)
    Capital stock:          5,000 billion
    Business inventories:   300 billion
    Inflation rate:         2.0%
    GDP growth rate:        2.5%
    Unemployment rate:      5.0% (equal to NAIRU)
    Central bank rate:      3.0%
    Mortgage rate:          5.5%
    Prime rate:             5.0%
    Government revenues:    400 billion
    Government expenditures:410 billion (slight deficit)
    Government debt:        800 billion (40% of GDP)
    Income tax rate:        25%
    Capital gains tax rate: 15%
    Excise tax rate:        10%


13. POLICY LEVERS
=================
The player can adjust five policy instruments between quarters:

  Lever                    Default   Range       Effect
  ---------------------    -------   ---------   ---------------------------
  Income Tax Rate          25%       5-50%       Higher rates increase revenue
                                                 but create tax drag on demand
  Capital Gains Tax Rate   15%       0-40%       Affects revenue from asset
                                                 market activity
  Excise Tax Rate          10%       0-25%       Consumption-based revenue;
                                                 mild demand drag
  Gov. Spending Target     410B      200-800B    Fiscal stimulus/austerity;
                                                 feeds into the IS curve
  Central Bank Rate        3.0%      0-12%       Primary monetary policy tool;
                                                 affects mortgage/prime rates,
                                                 debt service costs, and
                                                 asset valuations
